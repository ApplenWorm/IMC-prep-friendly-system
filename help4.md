Here’s a **comprehensive preparation plan** for the **International Mathematics Competition for University Students (IMC)** (the UK-based annual undergraduate contest), including **topics**, **high-quality book sources**, **structured study pathways**, and **practice/problem collections**. IMC problems are similar in nature to **Putnam-level** university mathematics problems and focus on **Algebra, Analysis (Real/Complex), Geometry, Number Theory, and Combinatorics** at the undergraduate level. ([imc-math.org.uk][1])

---

## 📌 IMC Core Content Areas

According to IMC organizers, typical problem domains include:

* **Algebra** — polynomials, linear/abstract algebraic manipulations
* **Analysis** — real (often elementary proof/inequalities) and complex techniques
* **Geometry** — plane geometry and occasionally advanced methods
* **Combinatorics & Number Theory** — counting, divisibility, modular arithmetic, Diophantine arguments ([imc-math.org.uk][2])

This parallels the **Putnam Competition** and other undergraduate contests, so preparatory material for Putnam also serves well for IMC. ([Stanford University][3])

## 📘 Core Problem-Solving Books (Olympiad & Undergraduate Level)

### **1. Problem Collections Closely Aligned with IMC/Putnam**

These books provide structured problems and solutions across competitive mathematics topics that also appear in IMC.

* **Putnam and Beyond** — Edited by Răzvan Gelca & Titu Andreescu — *comprehensive undergraduate problem collection covering algebra, analysis, number theory, and combinatorics*, with complete solutions. ([Google Books][4])
* **Berkeley Problems in Mathematics** — de Souza & Silva — *high-quality competitive problem sets with solution sketches*, suitable for collegiate level (you already have this). ([Google Sites][5])
* **Winning Solutions** — Lozansky & Rousseau — *a classic collection from Putnam/other contests* (solution approach and strategic depth). ([Stanford University][3])
* **Problem-Solving Through Problems** — Loren C. Larson — *strong emphasis on methodical solving approaches across topics*. ([Stanford University][3])
* **Problem-Solving Strategies** — Arthur Engel — *toolkit of general approaches; good for cross-topic thinking*. ([Stanford University][3])

---

### **2. Subject-Specific Theory + Problems**

After you’re comfortable with general problem solving, drill into these:

#### **Algebra**

* *Functional Equations and How to Solve Them* — Christopher Small — *solves common topic in undergrad contests*. ([MathOverflow][6])
* Core undergraduate algebra texts (e.g., Lang/Artin) for foundations.

#### **Analysis**

* Standard Real Analysis (e.g., **Baby Rudin** or **Apostol**) — *understanding proof techniques used in contest problems*.

#### **Number Theory**

* *104 Number Theory Problems* — Andreescu et al. — *classic olympiad number theory* (useful beyond high school level). ([Stanford University][3])
* Other university modular arithmetic/Diophantine collections.

#### **Combinatorics**

* *A Path to Combinatorics for Undergraduates* – Andreescu & Engel — *topic-oriented problem sets* (useful for contest style). ([Reddit][7])
* *Olympiad Combinatorics* — Pranav Sriram — *structured problems*. ([Reddit][7])

#### **Geometry**

* *Geometry Unbound* — Kiran Kedlaya — *modern, problem-oriented geometry serviceable for high-level contests*. ([MathOverflow][6])
* Classic Euclidean collections (Coxeter, Yaglom).

---

### **3. Strategic/Problem-Solving Mindset**

These books help bridge theory and contest thinking.

* **How to Solve It** — George Pólya — *methodology for tackling novel problems*. ([Wikipedia][8])
* **The Art and Craft of Problem Solving** — Paul Zeitz — *develops heuristic thinking*. ([Crown][9])

## 📅 Structured Preparation Plan (6–12 Months)

### **Phase 1 — Foundations (Months 1–2)**

Goal: Ensure solid understanding of undergraduate mathematics fundamentals.

1. **Real & Complex Analysis Basics:**

   * Master proof techniques (ε-δ arguments, mean value/integration basics).
   * Work simple analysis problems.
2. **Algebra Fundamentals:**

   * Revise polynomials, systems of equations, linear algebra basics.
3. **Basic Combinatorics & Number Theory:**

   * Permutations, combinations, divisibility, modular arithmetic.

**Materials**: Standard textbooks in these areas + *Pólya’s How to Solve It*.

---

### **Phase 2 — Core Problem Practice (Months 3–6)**

Goal: Build problem-solving stamina and familiarity.

1. Work through chapters of **Putnam and Beyond** systematically by topic.
2. Alternate with sections of *Problem-Solving Through Problems* and *Problem-Solving Strategies*.
3. Track difficult topics (e.g., inequalities, functional equations) and revisit fundamentals.

**Deliverables**:

* Topic-wise notebook with solutions and common techniques.
* Weekly summaries of new problem types learned.

---

### **Phase 3 — Targeted IMC/Putnam Drill (Months 7–10)**

Goal: Simulate competition conditions and tackle past problems.

1. **Download and solve past IMC problems with timing conditions** — available on the official IMC site). ([imc-math.org.uk][10])
2. Do sections from Putnam archives that mimic IMC style.
3. Pair practice with detailed solution writeups (clear proofs a priority).

---

### **Phase 4 — Review & Mock Competitions (Months 10–12)**

Goal: Consolidate skills under timed conditions.

1. Set full “2-day” problem sessions with problems drawn from IMC/Putnam.
2. Evaluate weak areas and revise topic fundamentals.
