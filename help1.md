This is a fantastic goal. The International Mathematics Competition for University Students (IMC) is incredibly prestigious and requires a deep, creative understanding of undergraduate mathematics. It’s a step up from standard university exams—it demands the rigor of a PhD qualifying exam combined with the lateral thinking of the IMO (International Mathematical Olympiad).

"Berkeley Problems in Mathematics" is a stellar starting point (it's essentially a collection of PhD qualifying exam problems), but for the IMC, you'll want resources that focus specifically on the *Olympiad flavor* of university math.

Here is a comprehensive, structured guide to the books, the prerequisites, and a concrete plan to get you competition-ready.

---

### Phase 1: The Foundation (What to study *before* doing Olympiad problems)

Before tackling IMC or Putnam-level problems, your theoretical foundation must be rock solid. You cannot solve a competition-level complex analysis problem if you don't intuitively understand contour integration or the Residue Theorem inside and out.

**Core Subjects to Master:**

1. **Linear Algebra:** This is heavily tested. You need to understand canonical forms (Jordan, Rational), spectral theory, inner product spaces, and matrix polynomials.
2. **Real Analysis:** Limits, continuity, sequences and series of functions, uniform convergence, metric spaces, and multivariable calculus.
3. **Complex Analysis:** Analytic functions, contour integration, Cauchy's theorems, singularities, and conformal mappings.
4. **Abstract Algebra:** Groups, rings, fields, and polynomials.
5. **Combinatorics & Probability:** Generating functions, recurrence relations, and graph theory.

**Recommended Foundational Materials:**

* **Video Lectures:** **MIT OpenCourseWare (OCW)** is your best friend here. Specifically, look up courses 18.100 (Real Analysis), 18.06 / 18.700 (Linear Algebra), and 18.04 (Complex Variables).
* **Theory Textbooks (Read these before problem-solving):**
* *Analysis:* "Principles of Mathematical Analysis" by Walter Rudin (often called "Baby Rudin").
* *Linear Algebra:* "Linear Algebra Done Right" by Sheldon Axler or "Linear Algebra" by Hoffman and Kunze (Hoffman/Kunze is tougher and closer to competition level).
* *Abstract Algebra:* "Abstract Algebra" by David S. Dummit and Richard M. Foote.



---

### Phase 2: The Olympiad Arsenal (General Problem Books)

Once the theory is locked in, you need to transition from "textbook exercises" to "competition problems." Since the US-based Putnam Competition is the closest equivalent to the IMC, Putnam resources are highly relevant.

* **"Putnam and Beyond" by Răzvan Gelca and Titu Andreescu:** *If you only buy one book from this entire list, make it this one.* It bridges the gap between undergraduate coursework and Olympiad-level problem-solving, neatly categorized by university topics (Analysis, Algebra, Geometry, etc.).
* **"Problem-Solving Through Problems" by Loren C. Larson:** A classic. It focuses heavily on heuristics at the undergraduate level.
* **"The William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition" (Various volumes):** There are volumes covering 1985–2000 and 2001–2016 (by Kedlaya, Poonen, Vakil, etc.). These contain real competition problems with incredibly detailed, multiple solutions.
* **"Problem-Solving Strategies" by Arthur Engel:** While technically aimed at high school IMO prep, its chapters on combinatorics, inequalities, and number theory are essential for the IMC.

---
### Phase 3: Subject-Specific Deep Dives

To get an edge, you will need to drill down into specific subjects using advanced problem books.

**1. Real and Complex Analysis:**

* **"Problems and Theorems in Analysis I and II" by George Pólya and Gábor Szegő:** This is a legendary masterpiece. It teaches analysis entirely through problem-solving. It is incredibly difficult but will build immense mathematical maturity.
* **"Problems in Mathematical Analysis" (Volumes 1, 2, and 3) by W.J. Kaczor and M.T. Nowak:** An exhaustive collection of problems covering everything from sequences to integration.

**2. Algebra (Linear and Abstract):**

* **"Linear Algebra Problem Book" by Paul R. Halmos:** Written in a unique "Socratic" style. It guides you through the deep concepts of linear algebra purely through questions and hints.
* **"Problems in Higher Algebra" by D.K. Faddeev and I.S. Sominsky:** A classic Russian text that contains a massive amount of matrix and polynomial problems, which are very common in the IMC.

**3. Combinatorics:**

* **"A Path to Combinatorics for Undergraduates" by Titu Andreescu and Zuming Feng:** Excellent for bridging the gap between basic counting and Olympiad-level generating functions and graph theory.

---

### Phase 4: Your Preparation Plan

Here is a roadmap to structure your studies leading up to the competition.

**Step 1: The Diagnostic & Theory Review (Months 1-2)**

* Print out a past IMC paper from the [official website](https://www.imc-math.org.uk/). Try to read through it.
* Identify the symbols or concepts you don't know.
* Spend these first two months watching MIT OCW lectures and reading Rudin/Hoffman to plug those theoretical gaps. Do the exercises at the back of the textbook chapters.

**Step 2: Topic Drilling (Months 3-5)**

* Dedicate specific days of the week to specific topics (e.g., Monday/Thursday for Analysis, Tuesday/Friday for Algebra, Wednesday for Combinatorics).
* Work primarily out of *Putnam and Beyond*.
* *Crucial Strategy:* Never look at the solution immediately. Struggle with a problem for at least 30-45 minutes. If you get stuck, write down *why* you are stuck. Finding out what you don't know is the most valuable part of the process.

**Step 3: The Crucible / Timed Practice (Month 6 to Competition)**

* Abandon the textbooks. Switch entirely to past IMC papers and past Putnam papers.
* **Simulate conditions:** Sit down in a quiet room for 5 hours (or whatever the specific paper dictates) with only a pen and paper.
* After the time is up, ruthlessly grade yourself. Read the official solutions and compare them to yours. Learn the "tricks" you missed (like a clever substitution in an integral or a specific matrix factorization).
* Keep a "notebook of tricks" where you write down clever algebraic manipulations or specific counter-examples you encountered. Review this notebook weekly.
