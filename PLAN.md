IMC PREPARATION MASTER PLAN
===========================

This `PLAN.md` is the central roadmap for your preparation for the **International Mathematics Competition for University Students (IMC)**. It is built entirely from the notes and resources in this repository (including `resource-from-docx.md`, the `help*.md` files, and your earlier guides) and is meant to be *actionable* rather than encyclopedic.

Use this together with:

- `RESOURCES.md` — full book and resource library with titles exactly as in your notes
- `TODO.md` — a checkable list of concrete tasks to complete phase by phase

---

## 1. Competition Overview (What You’re Aiming At)

- **Competition**: International Mathematics Competition for University Students (IMC)
- **Site**: `imc-math.org.uk`
- **Format**: Two **5-hour** exams on consecutive days, **5 problems per day**, proof-based
- **Core Areas**:
  - **Algebra** — linear algebra (matrices, eigenvalues, canonical forms), abstract algebra (groups, rings, fields, modules)
  - **Real & Complex Analysis** — sequences, series, limits, continuity, differentiation, integration, metric spaces, complex functions
  - **Combinatorics** — counting, graph theory, combinatorial identities, pigeonhole, probabilistic method
  - **Geometry** — Euclidean geometry, convex sets, geometric inequalities (less dominant than the above three)
- **Scoring**: 10 points per problem; medals roughly:
  - First Prize: top ~10%
  - Second Prize: next ~20%
  - Third Prize: next ~30%
- **Medal heuristic**: Solving **2–3 full problems** across the two days usually yields a medal. The **Problem 1** on each day is designed to be the most accessible and should be a priority target.

---

## 2. Philosophy of Your Preparation

1. **Theory before tricks**: You will *not* jump straight into olympiad problems. For each topic, you will:
   - First: build rigorous theory from textbooks like **“Linear Algebra Done Right (3rd ed.)” by Sheldon Axler** and **“Understanding Analysis (2nd ed.)” by Stephen Abbott**.
   - Then: move to problem books like **“Putnam and Beyond (2nd ed.)” by Razvan Gelca & Titu Andreescu** and **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)” by Paulo Ney de Souza & Jorge-Nuno Silva**.

2. **Depth over breadth**: It is better to know **1–2 key books per subject deeply** than to skim many. This plan repeatedly emphasizes a small, canonical subset:
   - Linear algebra: **“Linear Algebra Done Right (3rd ed.)”**, **“Problems and Theorems in Linear Algebra”**, **“Linear Algebra (2nd ed.)”**
   - Real analysis: **“Understanding Analysis (2nd ed.)”**, **“Principles of Mathematical Analysis (3rd ed.)”**, **“Problems in Real Analysis: Advanced Calculus on the Real Axis”**
   - Core competitions: **“Putnam and Beyond (2nd ed.)”**, **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)”**

3. **Structured phases**: Your year is divided into **phases** (0–5), each with:
   - Clear **time window**
   - Exact **books to read**
   - A **problem workload**

4. **Brutal honesty via diagnostics**: You will periodically test yourself using:
   - **IMC 2023, Day 1, Problems 1–2**
   - Random problems from **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)”**
   - Recent **Putnam** problems

   and adjust your phase (accelerate or repeat) based on performance.

## 4. Phased 21‑Week Preparation Plan (to 27 July 2026)

You have **just under 21 weeks** until the IMC (around **27 July 2026**). The phases below are compressed into that window, assuming roughly **15–20 focused hours per week**.  
If some items are too dense, prioritize: **Linear Algebra → Real Analysis → IMC past problems**.

### Phase 0 (Weeks 1–2) — Mathematical Maturity & Proof Fluency

**Goal**: Ensure you can read and write rigorous proofs comfortably and handle basic epsilon–delta arguments and algebraic manipulations.

- **Primary reading**:
  - **“How to Solve It” by George Pólya**
  - First rigorous chapters of **“Understanding Analysis (2nd ed.)” by Stephen Abbott** or **“Principles of Mathematical Analysis (3rd ed.)” by Walter Rudin**
  - Selected easier problems from **“The Art and Craft of Problem Solving (3rd ed.)” by Paul Zeitz**

- **Concrete outcomes**:
  - You can systematically use: induction, contradiction, contrapositive, pigeonhole, extremal principle, invariants.
  - You can write a clean proof of things like Bolzano–Weierstrass, Cauchy–Schwarz, rank–nullity, etc., without heavy guidance.

### Phase 1 (Weeks 3–8) — Foundations I: Linear Algebra & Real Analysis

**Goal**: Make **linear algebra** and **real analysis** your strongest areas. These appear in almost every IMC paper.

#### Linear Algebra Track

- **Theory**:
  - **“Linear Algebra Done Right (3rd ed.)” by Sheldon Axler** (primary)
  - **“Linear Algebra (2nd ed.)” by Kenneth Hoffman & Ray Kunze** (for determinants, rational canonical form, more computational aspects)
- **Problem book**:
  - **“Problems and Theorems in Linear Algebra” by V.V. Prasolov**
- **Core topics**:
  - Vector spaces, subspaces, basis, dimension
  - Linear maps, kernels, images, rank–nullity theorem
  - Eigenvalues/eigenvectors, diagonalization, minimal and characteristic polynomials
  - **Jordan canonical form** (at least conceptually) and **spectral theorem**
  - Inner product spaces, Gram–Schmidt, orthogonal projections
  - Cayley–Hamilton theorem, invariant subspaces, commuting matrices
- **Weekly target**:
  - 2–3 theory sessions (Axler/Hoffman–Kunze)
  - 2 problem sessions with **Prasolov** (5–10 problems each)

#### Real Analysis Track

- **Theory**:
  - **“Understanding Analysis (2nd ed.)” by Stephen Abbott** (primary)
  - **“Principles of Mathematical Analysis (3rd ed.)” by Walter Rudin**
  - Optionally: **“Mathematical Analysis (2nd ed.)” by Tom Apostol**
- **Problem book**:
  - **“Problems in Real Analysis: Advanced Calculus on the Real Axis” by Radulescu, Radulescu & Andreescu**
  - (Later) **“Problems in Mathematical Analysis, Vols. 1–3” by W.J. Kaczor & M.T. Nowak**
- **Core topics**:
  - Sequences, series, convergence tests
  - Continuity, uniform continuity, intermediate and extreme value theorems
  - Differentiation: mean value theorem, Taylor’s theorem with remainder
  - Riemann integration and improper integrals
  - Sequences/series of functions, uniform convergence, Weierstrass M‑test
  - Metric spaces, completeness, compactness, connectedness (at least in \(\mathbb{R}^n\))
- **Weekly target**:
  - 2 theory sessions (Abbott then Rudin)
  - 2 problem sessions (Radulescu/Radulescu/Andreescu, then Kaczor–Nowak)

**End‑of‑Phase 1 diagnostic**:

- Try a few **linear algebra and analysis problems** from:
  - **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)” by Paulo Ney de Souza & Jorge-Nuno Silva**
  - **IMC past papers** Problem 1/2 types
- If you can solve several with full proofs, you are ready for Phase 2.

### Phase 2 (Weeks 9–13) — Foundations II: Abstract Algebra, Complex Analysis, Combinatorics

**Goal**: Build solid but focused theory in the remaining key domains.

#### Abstract Algebra

- **Theory**:
  - **“Abstract Algebra (3rd ed.)” by Dummit & Foote**
  - **“Topics in Algebra (2nd ed.)” by I.N. Herstein**
  - **“Algebra” by Michael Artin**
- **Core topics**:
  - Groups: subgroups, cosets, homomorphisms, isomorphisms, Lagrange’s theorem
  - Group actions, orbits, stabilizers, Burnside’s lemma, Sylow theorems
  - Rings and ideals, quotient rings, polynomial rings
  - Fields, basic field extensions, finite fields
  - Modules (at least enough to understand deeper linear algebra structure)

#### Complex Analysis

- **Theory**:
  - **“Complex Analysis” by Lars Ahlfors**
  - **“Complex Analysis (3rd ed.)” by Joseph Bak & Donald Newman**
  - **“Visual Complex Analysis” by Tristan Needham** (for intuition)
- **Competition‑oriented**:
  - **“Complex Numbers from A to Z” by Titu Andreescu & Dorin Andrica**
  - **“Problems and Solutions for Complex Analysis” by Rami Shakarchi**
  - **“A Collection of Problems on Complex Analysis” by L.I. Volkovyskii et al.**
- **Core topics**:
  - Analytic (holomorphic) functions and Cauchy–Riemann equations
  - Cauchy’s integral theorem and formula
  - Power series, Taylor and Laurent series
  - Isolated singularities, residues, contour integration
  - Maximum modulus principle, Liouville’s theorem, Rouché’s theorem

#### Combinatorics

- **Theory**:
  - **“A Walk Through Combinatorics (4th ed.)” by Miklós Bóna**
  - **“Combinatorics: Topics, Techniques, Algorithms” by Peter Cameron**
- **Problem‑oriented**:
  - **“Generatingfunctionology (3rd ed.)” by Herbert Wilf**
  - **“102 Combinatorial Problems from the Training of the USA IMO Team” by Titu Andreescu & Zuming Feng**
  - **“Problems in Combinatorics and Graph Theory” by Ioan Tomescu**
- **Core topics**:
  - Counting: permutations, combinations, inclusion–exclusion
  - Generating functions (ordinary and exponential)
  - Graph theory: trees, connectivity, matchings, Ramsey basics
  - Pigeonhole principle (simple and sophisticated forms)
  - Recurrence relations, probabilistic method (basic)

### Phase 3 (Weeks 14–17) — Bridging Theory to Competition

**Goal**: Move from textbook understanding to **olympiad‑style** problem solving.

- **Main engines**:
  - **“Putnam and Beyond (2nd ed.)” by Razvan Gelca & Titu Andreescu**
  - **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)” by Paulo Ney de Souza & Jorge-Nuno Silva**
- **Supporting problem‑solving texts**:
  - **“Problem-Solving Through Problems” by Loren C. Larson**
  - **“Problem-Solving Strategies” by Arthur Engel**
  - **“The Art and Craft of Problem Solving (3rd ed.)” by Paul Zeitz**
  - **“How to Solve It” by George Pólya** (revisited)

**Weekly pattern** (approximate):

- 2 days: Problems from **Putnam and Beyond (2nd ed.)** in the topic you have just studied (e.g., linear algebra or analysis)
- 1 day: Problems from **Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)**
- 1 day: Problems from **Problem-Solving Through Problems** or **Problem-Solving Strategies**
- 1 day: Theory review (from Axler, Abbott, Rudin, etc.) to patch weaknesses found through problems

**Process for each problem**:

1. Spend **at least 45 minutes** on a problem before looking at a solution.
2. Write down every attempt, idea, and dead end.
3. When reading the solution, explicitly identify the **key insight** and write it into your **techniques notebook**.
4. The next day, try to re‑derive the solution from scratch without looking.

### Phase 4 (Weeks 18–21) — IMC Past Papers & Full Simulations

**Goal**: Align yourself specifically with **IMC style** and build time‑management and endurance.

- **Main sources**:
  - **IMC official site (imc-math.org.uk)** — problems and solutions from 1994 onward
  - **The William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition 1985–2000: Problems, Solutions, and Commentary** by Kedlaya, Poonen & Vakil
  - **The William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition 2001–2016: Problems, Solutions, and Commentary** by Kedlaya, Kane, Kane & O’Dorney
  - **The William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition: Problems and Solutions 1965–1984** by Alexanderson, Klosinski & Larson

**Simulation routine**:

- Aim for **2 full IMC exams per week**:
  - 5 problems, 5 hours, no notes, no internet, no calculators.
  - Sit in silence; replicate real‑competition conditions.
- After each simulation:
  - Grade yourself honestly against official solutions.
  - For each unsolved or partially solved problem:
    - Read the solution carefully.
    - Write a one‑page reconstruction of the solution in your own words.
    - Extract any *new trick* into your **techniques notebook**.

You may mix in **Vojtech Jarník** and **Putnam** past papers to diversify problem styles.

---

### Phase 5 — Advanced Deepening & Weak‑Spot Attack (After IMC / Long Term)

**Goal**: Push your ceiling up and eliminate weak areas before the real contest.

- **Advanced resources**:
  - **“Problems from the Book (2nd ed.)” by Titu Andreescu & Gabriel Dospinescu**
  - **“Problems and Theorems in Analysis, Vols. I & II” by George Pólya & Gabor Szegő**
  - **“Contests in Higher Mathematics: Miklós Schweitzer Competitions 1962–1991” edited by Gabor J. Szekely**
  - Selected chapters from **“Matrix Analysis” by Roger Horn & Charles Johnson**
  - Selected chapters from **“The Probabilistic Method (4th ed.)” by Alon & Spencer**

**Strategy**:

- Identify your **two weakest topics** (e.g., polynomial algebra, complex integrals, generating functions).
- For each weak topic:
  - Choose an advanced resource (e.g., **“Polynomials” by Victor Prasolov** for polynomial problems).
  - Spend 2–3 weeks attacking as many problems as possible.
- Keep at least **one lighter day per week** to avoid burnout; re‑read inspiring material like **“Proofs from THE BOOK (6th ed.)” by Martin Aigner & Günter Ziegler** or **“The Art of Mathematics: Coffee Time in Memphis” by Béla Bollobás**.

## 5. Weekly Study Template

During **theory‑heavy phases (0–2)** (~10–15 hours/week):

- **Day 1–2**: Theory (textbook reading, proofs, note‑taking)
- **Day 3–4**: Textbook exercises (non‑competition problems)
- **Day 5**: Light reading of **“How to Solve It”** or **“The Art and Craft of Problem Solving”**
- **Day 6–7**: 3–5 competition‑level problems from topics you have already covered

During **competition‑heavy phases (3–5)** (~12–18 hours/week):

- **3 days**: Problems from **“Putnam and Beyond (2nd ed.)”**, **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)”**, **Pólya–Szegő**, Kaczor–Nowak
- **1 day**: Past papers (IMC, Vojtech Jarník, Putnam)
- **1 day**: Reviewing difficult solutions and updating your techniques notebook
- **1 day**: Focused theory review of weak spots

## 6. Diagnostics: Where You Should Enter the Plan

Use these tests to decide your current phase:

- **Test A (IMC 2023 Day 1)**:
  - If you can solve **Problem 1 and Problem 2** within 3 hours total, you can start directly in **Phase 3** for those topics.
- **Test B (Berkeley Problems)**:
  - Try **5 random problems** from **“Berkeley Problems in Mathematics (3rd ed.)”**.
  - If you solve **3 or more**, you’re at least **Phase 2–3** in those areas.
- **Test C (Putnam)**:
  - Choose **5 problems** from a recent Putnam paper.
  - 0–1 solved → **Phase 1** level.
  - 2–3 solved → **Phase 2**.
  - 4–5 solved → **Phase 3+**.

You do **not** need to move all topics together; e.g. you might be Phase 3 in linear algebra but Phase 1 in complex analysis.

## 7. Techniques Notebook & Meta‑Skills

Throughout all phases, maintain a **techniques notebook** (physical or digital) with:

- A short description of each *new technique* you encounter  
  (e.g., “using generating functions to solve linear recurrences”, “Cauchy–Schwarz in disguise”, “using eigenvalues to count walks in a graph”).
- A **model problem** where that technique is used.
- A short reflection: *“How would I have thought of this? What hint suggests this tool?”*

Review this notebook every **2 weeks**. Over time it becomes your personal **“mini Putnam and Beyond”**.
